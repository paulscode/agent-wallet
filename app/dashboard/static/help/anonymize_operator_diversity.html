Operator diversity for on-chain Anonymize

Why the wizard caps single-operator on-chain sessions at the moderate tier, what's still protected, and what configuring a second operator buys you.

TL;DR. The wallet's bundled operators.json ships three operators: canonical Boltz, Middleway, and Eldamar. The default chain puts canonical Boltz on the reverse leg (where the destination address is exposed) and Middleway → Eldamar on the submarine leg with a pre-funding fallback chain. If both alts are unreachable at quote time, the wizard offers single-operator Boltz at the cost of capping the tier at moderate. All other privacy defenses — Tor stream isolation, amount binning, MPP fragmentation, randomized inter-leg delay, optional Liquid hop — apply on every session regardless of the operator pair.

What an on-chain anonymize session looks like

An on-chain anonymize session has two halves:

  1. Submarine swap. Your on-chain BTC funds a lockup address controlled by Boltz operator A; operator A pays you Lightning sats.
  2. Reverse swap. You pay Lightning sats to operator B; operator B sends BTC to your destination address.

If operator A and operator B are the same entity, their logs contain both halves: the input UTXO that funded the submarine lockup, the destination address from the reverse-swap request, and the approximate timing + binned amount of both. Two genuinely independent operators each only see one half.

What's still mitigated with one operator

The wallet's other defenses don't go away when only one operator is configured. With a single operator the following are still active on every on-chain session:

DefenseEffect
Tor stream isolation The submarine and reverse legs run on different SOCKS listeners with fresh Tor circuits. The operator can't link the two requests by circuit identity.
Pinned-JA4 HTTP client Both legs share an identical, fingerprint-pinned TLS handshake. The TLS layer doesn't differentiate the two calls.
Per-call internal-ID strip Neither leg's request body carries session IDs, quote tokens, or other wallet-internal identifiers.
Amount binning Both legs use a bin amount, not your exact request, so amount-based matching has many candidates.
Inter-leg delay (6–48 h) The two legs are separated by a randomized multi-hour gap, weakening timing correlation.
Priv-channel hop Lightning-side traffic routes through a fresh throwaway private channel, hiding your primary Lightning node identity from the operator.
MPP fragmentation Lightning payments are split across paths, weakening any "this LN node paid me" signal.
Liquid round-trip hop Interposes a Confidential-Transactions-blinded L-BTC dwell between the two legs (when enabled).

A single-operator session with all of these active is meaningfully better than no mixing — an attacker has to actively correlate amounts, timings, and destination addresses against the operator's own volume to break the link. The operator has the data to attempt this; the wallet's job is to make the work hard and the result probabilistic.

What the wallet can't defend against in software

Two things genuinely require operator diversity:

  1. A logging-then-correlating operator. If Boltz (or any single operator) chooses to log both halves and run a correlation pipeline on its own data, no in-wallet mitigation breaks the link. The wallet can raise the noise floor but can't make the data unavailable.
  2. A subpoenaed or compromised operator. Logs the operator keeps for legitimate operational reasons (anti-abuse, support tickets, fee accounting) become correlation evidence if a future legal compulsion or breach exposes them.

For both, two independent operators is the only defense — neither alone has the full picture.

How to add a second operator

Set both BOLTZ_SUBMARINE_ONION_URL and BOLTZ_REVERSE_ONION_URL in your .env to distinct onion URLs of two independent Boltz-compatible operators. The wizard re-renders the tier badge and removes the banner without a redeploy.

Picking a non-canonical operator deserves diligence — onion authenticity, Lightning liquidity in your bin range, contact responsiveness, backend version, uptime history.
